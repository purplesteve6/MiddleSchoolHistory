Background

John Locke was an English philosopher during the Enlightenment who focused on the structure of governments. Locke believed that people were naturally kind and, when given the choice, would help others before helping themselves. Because people are naturally good at heart, Locke believed that strict government was not necessary.

According to Locke, people could work out problems by openly compromising with one another. Government, he argued, should exist to protect people‚Äôs rights‚Äînot to control every part of their lives.

Two Treatises of Government (1690)

In his famous book on government, Two Treatises of Government (1690), Locke wrote about the relationship between government and its people. He challenged the idea that kings should rule with unlimited power and argued that governments exist because people allow them to exist.

Locke believed government power comes from the consent of the governed. If a government fails to protect the people, then the people have the right to change or replace it.

Many kings and rulers feared Locke‚Äôs ideas‚Äîbecause he argued that people could replace a government that refused to protect their rights.

Natural Rights

Locke wrote about what he called natural rights‚Äîrights that are granted to you by nature (or God) upon birth. These rights are not given by government and therefore cannot be taken away by government.

According to Locke, the natural rights of all people are the right to life, liberty, and the right to own property. The main job of government, Locke argued, is to protect these rights.

Influence on the Declaration of Independence

In 1776, Thomas Jefferson borrowed Locke‚Äôs ideas when writing the Declaration of Independence. Jefferson wrote that all people have inalienable rights‚Äîrights that cannot be taken away‚Äîsuch as life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness.

Although Jefferson changed Locke‚Äôs wording slightly, the main idea stayed the same: governments exist to protect rights, not to grant them.
